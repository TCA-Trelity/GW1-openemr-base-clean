Part 00 CXO lens

Executive summary

This repository is two things at once, and this document covers both at equal depth: a mature open-source electronic health record platform (OpenEMR), and an AI clinical co-pilot built as an additive layer on top of it. Understanding the product means understanding how the new layer rides safely on the old platform without changing it.

The platform. OpenEMR is a Free and Open Source EHR and medical practice-management system — fully integrated records, scheduling, and billing — in production use worldwide, ONC-certified, and backed by a large developer community. This fork is built on OpenEMR 8.2.0-dev: PHP 8.2+, a FHIR R4 US Core API, OAuth2 / SMART-on-FHIR, a modern custom-module system, and a real audit-logging subsystem. It is a serious, security-reviewed foundation — and a 20-year-old monolith with the constraints that history brings (detailed in Part 02).

The product. The AgentForge Clinical Co-Pilot answers a specific, expensive problem for one specific user — a high-volume retina surgeon. Its thesis is that the physician's pain is a presentation failure, not a data failure: the record already holds what the doctor needs, buried in clutter that exists for verified reasons (billing, certification, configurability). So the co-pilot precomputes a single, cited, one-page brief during the 5–20 minutes a patient waits after check-in, and serves it instantly at the door.

The one design idea

Move the thinking to where time is free. Completeness is bought at preparation time, where minutes are available; speed is spent at read time, where only seconds are. That single move dissolves the speed-vs-completeness tradeoff — and it is the only shape the platform allows, because OpenEMR has no async job queue, so heavy work must live outside the EHR.

How trust is built. Nothing here rests on trusting the model to behave. A deterministic citation gate — plain code, not a model — sits between generation and display; any claim without a real source is blocked before the doctor sees it. Clinical calculations are hand-checkable arithmetic the model presents but never performs. And because our security audit found OpenEMR's per-patient access check unimplemented, the co-pilot constructs the patient-level access control it cannot inherit, rather than trusting the platform to provide it. The governing rule throughout: the system may be unavailable; it may never be silently wrong.

Where it stands, honestly. This is a working demo on synthetic data — not a deployed clinical system. The architecture is committed and substantially built; several steps remain before a physician could rely on it (a live browser SMART launch, a de-identification pipeline for real records, high-availability hosting, and clinical validation of what the brief chooses to omit). Those are enumerated plainly in Part 11, each with its status. This candor is deliberate — it mirrors the repository's own engineering posture.

For the CTO / CIO

Upgrade-safe by design

The EHR core is untouched. The AI is a sidecar talking only through FHIR and OAuth2/SMART — it scales, fails, and upgrades independently of the monolith.

For the CISO

Constructed access control

Patient-bound SMART tokens, a fail-closed policy-enforcement point, and defense-in-depth audit logging — the control the platform lacks is built, not assumed.

For the CMO / physician

Provenance, not opinion

Every fact cites its source; the tool surfaces facts and computations but does not diagnose or prescribe. The doctor stays responsible for the answer.

Part 01 Product lens

The problem, and the one user it's built for

We deliberately reject the failed thesis that "physicians need help finding information." The product is built for one specific person in one specific workflow — and that constraint is what makes it good.

The user is Dan, a retina surgeon of ~35 years at a ~30-surgeon practice inside a ~300-physician retina network. He founded the EHR that became the category leader in ophthalmology — which makes him simultaneously the target user, the domain's founding builder, and the harshest critic the design will ever face. He is not AI-averse: he already pastes chart screenshots into a chatbot between rooms because it is "so incredibly valuable" — while insisting the physician stays responsible for the answer.

What he needs in the 30 seconds before he opens the door: who is this, why are they here, what changed since last time, what's on file, and what actually matters today. The chart holds all of it — but at equal weight, on an overcrowded screen, so he digs for it manually, on the clock, seventy times a day.

Why a generic co-pilot fails him — what ophthalmology specifically demands

Imaging-first

The scan is the story

Follow-ups are read off images more than text, yet imaging software is disconnected from the record and shows the same views regardless of disease. Fast access to the right scans, in context, is core surface — not a roadmap item.

Laterality

Everything has a side

Findings, procedures, and risks attach to the left eye, right eye, or both (OD / OS / OU). Laterality must be a first-class attribute on every fact, never buried in prose.

Cadence math

Chronic care runs on arithmetic

Treat-and-extend injection intervals and cumulative drug-toxicity monitoring (hydroxychloroquine dose × time) are longitudinal calculations Dan recomputes by hand every visit — "it scares me to death that I'm missing a subtle toxicity."

Fragmented sources

Referral, elderly, multi-source

Patients arrive by referral with fragmented, sometimes self-contradicting records. Per-fact provenance and verification status matter from day one.

You've got something in here — what she's hoping for… it's not a billing field… but it's really important. Dan, on patient goals as first-class clinical data

The seven jobs the co-pilot does (each traced to a real need)

Every capability points to an observed moment in Dan's day. The unifying reason it's a conversational agent and not a dashboard: a fixed layout shows everything at equal weight — which is the 20-page template he hates. Only an agent can make the per-patient, per-visit relevance judgment a fixed layout structurally cannot.

UC-1 · the anti-template

The 90-second brief

One precomputed, triaged, cited page: why they're here, what changed, active problems & meds, laterality-tagged findings, today's risk flags, and what they're hoping for.

UC-2 · a thread, not a query

Iterative drill-down

Multi-turn chat over prepared facts — "was that allergy ever verified?", "when did they start that med?" — each answer setting up the next; verifications persist.

UC-3 · reasoning, not sorting

Contradiction surfacing

Detects cross-source disagreements (two med start-dates; an allergy asserted then denied), ranks by clinical severity, and names the trustworthy source.

UC-4 · the flagship

Auto-computed toxicity risk

Deterministic cumulative-dose × duration vs. published thresholds — the calculation Dan does by hand every visit, now read instead of derived.

UC-5 · longitudinal

Treat-and-extend guidance

Correlates each imaging outcome with the interval since the last injection: "stable at 8 weeks, leaked at 12 — recommend 8-week interval."

UC-6 · one toggle away

Imaging in context

Pre-selected, pre-fetched scans stamped with clinical context ("6 weeks after the last Avastin injection") — closing the imaging↔record disconnect.

UC-7 · care, not just billing

Patient-goal-aware care. Surfaces goals as first-class, verified facts — "a mother who wants to be back in contact lenses in two weeks for her daughter's wedding photos." A treatment plan reads differently when the system carries what the patient actually cares about, information that lives in intake prose, not billing fields.

What the agent will refuse to do (non-goals)

  • It does not diagnose or prescribe. It surfaces facts and computations and cites them; the physician decides. (Trust posture and the regulatory not-CDS line.)
  • It does not write to the medical record beyond recording fact verifications — no orders, no note authorship in this scope.
  • It does not answer generic medical questions untethered from this chart — it is a co-pilot for this patient, not a medical search engine.
  • It does not reach beyond the launch patient — structurally, via a patient-bound credential, not by policy.
Part 02 Platform · technical + product

The platform underneath: OpenEMR

Before the AI, there is the EHR. OpenEMR is a Free and Open Source electronic health record and medical practice-management system — integrated clinical records, scheduling, and billing — in production use worldwide, ONC-certified, and maintained by a large developer community. The co-pilot's central design promise ("change what the doctor sees, never how the record is stored") only means something because this platform is real, load-bearing, and worth leaving intact.

How a request actually flows

There are two distinct paths, reflecting a codebase two decades into its life and years into a modernization:

  • The legacy web UI — every interface page includes interface/globals.php, which loads the autoloader, library/auth.inc.php (session/auth), and the SQL layer. This is the classic "include globals at the top of every page" pattern across 928 interface files and 524 library files.
  • The modern API kernel — apis/dispatch.php builds a Symfony HttpKernel driven by an ordered chain of event subscribers: exception handling, telemetry, API-response logging, site setup, CORS, then two authorization listeners (OAuth2, then role/ACL), route extension, and view rendering. Authorization here is emphatically not UI-only.

The framework mix mirrors the split: Symfony 7.3 components (HttpKernel, EventDispatcher, DI, Routing, Console) power the modern kernel and the module event bus; Laminas MVC 3.8 drives 14 older "Zend modules"; templating is mid-migration from Smarty (27 templates, shrinking) to Twig (284 templates, dominant). The data layer stacks three deep: the legacy procedural API (sqlStatement(), sqlQuery()) delegates to the modern QueryUtils, which runs on ADODB — and OpenEMR's custom mysqli_log ADODB driver is exactly where automatic SQL auditing is injected. Doctrine DBAL 4 exists as a newer parallel surface.

The two integration points the co-pilot depends on

FHIR + OAuth2 / SMART

A real interoperability surface

A FHIR R4 US Core API (71 routes, 138 resource classes), OAuth2 with four grant types including SMART Backend Services (client-credentials), dynamic client registration (RFC 7591), token introspection (RFC 7662), and SMART EHR-launch. This is how the sidecar reads the record and how it obtains scoped credentials.

Modules + events

First-class UI extension

A DB-registered custom-module system (modules table → each module's openemr.bootstrap.php) on a Symfony EventDispatcher bus. Patient-chart cards inject via SectionEvent / RenderEvent — the co-pilot's brief card rides these, touching no core data structures.

What the pre-build audit found — the honest platform picture

This fork was security- and architecture-audited before the co-pilot was designed (AUDIT.md). The audit is deliberately candid, and the findings shaped the product rather than being papered over. Four matter to an adopter:

Finding S1 · Critical · the design-shaping one

OpenEMR's per-patient access check is unimplemented. BearerTokenAuthorizationStrategy::checkUserHasAccessToPatient() is a hardcoded return true; with a TODO. The phpGACL layer enforces section/role permissions correctly (e.g. patients|med), but there is no provider-to-patient-panel scoping — any authenticated clinician can read any patient through the API. This is upstream and load-bearing, so the co-pilot does not patch core; it constructs the patient-level control it cannot inherit (Part 04).

Performance · architectural

No async job queue

symfony/messenger is absent; background work is cron + synchronous listeners only. Long AI work cannot run inside the EHR — which is precisely why preparation lives in a sidecar-owned queue and the in-EHR listener only fires a ≤250 ms notification.

Security · fixed

Exception-message disclosure

The top-level API dispatcher once returned raw $e->getMessage() in 500s (a SQL/path-leak risk, against the repo's own standard). fixed — it now returns a generic message and logs detail server-side only.

Data quality · mitigated

Thin sample data

Stock ships 14 demographics-only patients and zero ophthalmology content, despite a full 17-table eye-exam schema existing. Any credible demo must seed its own realistic, messy corpus — which this fork does, doubling it as eval ground truth.

What the audit found strong

Defense-in-depth API authorization and real audit logging. Every API route runs both an OAuth2 scope check and a phpGACL role/section ACL. Access is genuinely audit-logged with user identity (api_log via a response-logger listener), token auth success/failure lands in the log table, and the fork adds a pluggable IHE-ATNA syslog sink plus a break-glass emergency-access concept — a stronger compliance foundation than stock. The co-pilot leans on this as a second, platform-native audit trail rather than rebuilding it.

Part 03 Architecture · technical

System architecture: a sidecar beside an untouched EHR

Three components, one untouched EHR. The AI runtime lives beside OpenEMR — not inside it — and talks to the record only through the official FHIR and OAuth2/SMART interfaces. This keeps the EHR upgrade-safe and gives the AI layer an independent failure domain and release cycle.

System of record

OpenEMR fork

Untouched core + one custom module (oe-module-clinical-copilot) that injects the brief card via SectionEvent/RenderEvent, registers the SMART app, and listens for check-in. No core data structures change.

The agent runtime

Node sidecar

Fastify + Zod. Owns the preparation pipeline & queue, the derived fact store, the chat loop, the citation gate, and the auth PEP. Exists as a separate service because OpenEMR can't run long AI work inline.

The doctor's surface

React panel

SMART-launched inside the patient chart, served same-origin by the sidecar, reusing the design system of the prototype the design partner validated (Tailwind/shadcn).

The latency model — the one idea that makes it fast

Clinic workflow contains a built-in gap: after the technician checks a patient in, they wait 5–20 minutes before the doctor enters. Every expensive operation runs in that gap. When the doctor opens the panel, serving the brief is a stored lookup — retrieval, not computation.

Part 04 FAQ · deep-dive

FAQ: How authorization works

The short version. OpenEMR checks role permissions correctly but leaves the per-patient check unimplemented, so the co-pilot does not inherit patient-level access control — it constructs it, with two credentials scoped to two tasks, enforced at one global gate that fails closed. The credential is the boundary, not the model's judgment.

The finding that forced this design (AUDIT.md S1)

OpenEMR's phpGACL layer enforces section/role ACLs correctly — patients|med, encounters|notes — and the API even runs OAuth2 scope and role ACL on every route. But checkUserHasAccessToPatient() returns true unconditionally. There is no provider-to-patient-panel scoping for staff, and superuser short-circuits to allow-all. Net: any authenticated clinician can read any patient through the API. We treat this as a fixed constraint of the platform, not a bug to patch in core — and build the missing control ourselves.

The dual-credential model

Interactive surface — brief + chat + scans

A patient-bound SMART token built

Activates from within one patient's chart and carries a SMART-on-FHIR EHR-launch token bound to exactly one patient and one logged-in user. If the agent is ever manipulated — a crafted question, malicious text inside a referral document — it structurally cannot reach another patient. The credential is the boundary.

Background preparer — before any doctor session

A read-only server credential built

Preparing today's schedule requires reading across the practice's patients, so the preparer holds a separate read-only client-credentials token. The honest tradeoff, mitigated: read-only, scoped to the preparation pipeline, never touching the interactive surface, and every access written to OpenEMR's own api_log with identity and timestamp.

Why each mechanism is there

Q Why dispatch on the JWT alg before anything else?

Because "algorithm confusion" is the classic JWT bypass: a verifier that will accept either RS256 or HS256 can be tricked into verifying an attacker-signed HS256 token using the public RSA key as an HMAC secret. The verifier dispatches strictly on alg: RS256 tokens are checked against OpenEMR's JWKS and then introspected; HS256 is only ever the demo dev-secret path. Two token types, two non-overlapping verification routes — an adversarial self-review (six attack lenses + independent verification) found no forgery, alg-confusion, cross-patient, or replay bypass.

Q Why introspect for the bound patient instead of reading it from the token?

The authoritative patient binding is not in the JWT — it is obtained from OpenEMR's /introspect endpoint after the signature verifies. That means the cross-patient 403 cannot be defeated by a client crafting or editing a claim: the value the gate compares against is the EHR's, not the caller's. This is what makes the boundary structural rather than advisory.

Q What happens when the system can't tell what role a real SMART user has?

It fails closed to the least-privileged role. Introspection carries no role, and an earlier version defaulted a real SMART user to physician — a fail-open risk. That was corrected: an underivable role now drops to nurse until a deployment wires real role derivation from verified OpenEMR user attributes (users.authorized + physician_type + gacl). The role gate can never fail open on the production path.

Q Does role change what the agent can do, or only what it can see?

Both. Role shapes capability. Verifying a medication, allergy, condition, clinical finding, imaging finding, or procedure requires a physician session; social history, family history, patient goals, and chief complaint are verifiable by delegated staff. Resident supervision (verify-then-countersign) slots into the same mechanism — designed, deferred past the demo. OpenEMR already knows physician-vs-technician, and the agent inherits it.

Q Isn't the passwordless dev-login a hole?

By design, and documented honestly (AUDIT.md S6). POST /api/dev-login is a passwordless credential issuer that lets a grader exercise the auth model without a full browser SMART launch. It is gated by DEV_LOGIN_SECRET being set at all (absent → 404) and must never be enabled in a real deployment — exactly like OpenEMR's own first-party ROPC password grant. Production authentication is SMART EHR-launch; what the demo proves is per-token patient binding and role enforcement, not access control on the demo login itself.

Q Why is enforcement off by default?

So the open, synthetic-data demo keeps working for anyone evaluating it, while the enforcement code path stays exercised by unit tests. AUTH_MODE=enforced flips on 401/403 rejection (activation runbook §D). Critically, the request path never 500s: in off mode a token that fails to verify is ignored and the request proceeds; in enforced mode an unexpected verifier error fails closed with 401, never leaking a 500.

Part 05 FAQ · security & regulatory

FAQ: Security & compliance

The posture rests on three ideas: keep PHI inside one boundary, treat every external document as hostile input, and stay clearly on the non-device side of the regulatory line. This week runs on synthetic data only, so no PHI leaves the boundary regardless — but the controls are built for when it will.

HIPAA · §164.312(b) audit controls

Two overlapping audit trails

OpenEMR's api_log records every API read with user + patient identity; the sidecar adds request-level correlation IDs so a full "who asked what, which records were touched" story reconstructs from logs alone. The fork also adds a pluggable IHE-ATNA syslog sink for SIEM export and a break-glass concept that forces logging even when otherwise disabled.

BAA · the only external transmission

Model access under agreement

The one crossing of the trust boundary is the model call, made under a Business Associate Agreement (Anthropic's Claude for Healthcare, Jan 2026, offers a click-to-accept BAA; equivalent BAA-covered access exists via AWS Bedrock, Google Vertex, Azure). One constraint carried forward: the strictest zero-retention configs exclude some newest tiers (which require 30-day retention), so model-tier and retention policy are chosen together in the pilot.

Prompt injection is treated as a certainty, not a possibility

Referral documents and patient-reported text are untrusted input — the agent processes them as data to be quoted and cited, never as instructions. The blast radius of a successful injection is capped structurally, by three independent limits: the credential reaches one patient only, the toolset has no write or outbound action, and the citation gate blocks fabricated output. Adversarial prompts — cross-patient access attempts, instruction injection via document content, extraction of data the requester shouldn't see — are standing regression tests, run on every change, not one-off checks.

Secrets, data minimization, and the regulatory line

  • Secrets live server-side only. The browser never holds a model-provider key; keys are environment configuration on the deployment host.
  • Observability is not a second PHI store. Logs and traces reference records by identifier, not content — Langfuse is self-hosted so traces never leave the boundary.
  • Hosting is honest about its phase. The demo runs on a PaaS (Railway) for speed; the pilot moves to BAA-capable infrastructure, and real-record ingestion goes behind a de-identification pipeline (the design partner's "strip PHI at the clinic edge" posture).
  • Not clinical-decision-support (the regulatory line). The co-pilot surfaces facts and computations and cites them; it does not state diagnoses or prescribe treatment. The physician drives — which keeps the tool clear of FDA CDS device regulation, matching the design partner's own guidance.
Inherited strength

Because the preparer reads through OpenEMR's audited API, its every access lands in the platform's own api_log — an independent, platform-native trail beside the sidecar's correlation-ID traces. The compliance story is built on two records that must agree, not one the co-pilot controls alone.

Part 06 FAQ · trust & safety

FAQ: Safety, trust & failure modes

The governing principle is a single sentence: the agent may only assert what the record supports; everything else must be phrased as absence or uncertainty. Trust is constructed — nothing here depends on the model choosing to behave.

How a claim earns its way onto the screen

Preparation extracts typed facts. Each one carries the metadata that lets a physician trust or challenge it at a glance:

The citation gate — code, not a model

Before anything reaches the screen, a deterministic checker confirms every citation resolves to a real record entry. An unsourced claim is blocked by construction and rewritten as absence — not trusted to the model's good behavior. This is why verification sits after generation and before display: the one point in the flow where every claim exists in final form but nothing has reached the user yet. Clinical rules are arithmetic, not opinion — hydroxychloroquine risk = daily dose × days vs. published thresholds; the model presents the result but never performs the calculation, so every number is hand-checkable.

The limitation, stated honestly

The gate guarantees provenance (every claim has a real source); it does not guarantee perfect interpretation (a model can still summarize a cited fact clumsily). Two mitigations: the source sits one click from every claim, and the eval suite measures faithfulness against ground truth. But the deeper worry is subtler:

The failure that worries us most is not a wrong fact — the gate catches those — but a correctly-cited brief that buried the one thing that mattered. ARCHITECTURE.md, on the triage blind spot

That is a triage failure, invisible to provenance checking, and measurable only by physician review of what the brief chose to omit. It is named as the top pre-reliance validation task (Part 11) — not hidden.

Failure modes: unavailable, never silently wrong

Preparation didn't run

Say so, with a timestamp

The panel shows the last-good preparation time — never a partial brief presented as complete. Partial coverage displays as partial; per-fact confidence carries the rest.

A tool or source fails mid-chat

Name the hole

The agent states what it couldn't reach instead of answering around it. Missing data displays as missing — clinically meaningful in itself.

Model provider down / rate-limited

Degrade to the prepared brief

Chat falls back to the already-prepared brief, which needs no live model, with retry and backoff behind the scenes.

EHR unreachable

Never block the doctor

The panel degrades to a link to the standard chart. The co-pilot's failure never blocks the clinical workflow.

Every demo is a test run

Synthetic patients ship with planted, ground-truthed contradictions (two conflicting medication start-dates with the recorded correct answer), so the same corpus seeds the demo and the test suite. Every test exercises a boundary (missing data, empty record, malformed input), an invariant (100% citation validity — guaranteed by construction, tested anyway), or a regression risk (cross-patient access, prompt injection via documents). Continuously measured: brief accuracy, citation-validity rate, refusal correctness, latency p50/p95 per surface, and cost per visit.

Part 07 FAQ · deep-dive

FAQ: Interoperability & the data model

The co-pilot reads the record through standard FHIR R4, never the database, and writes its prepared facts to a derived view that is never a second source of truth. Two design consequences fall out of one-patient scope: retrieval becomes an exact lookup, and rollback becomes safe by construction.

Why FHIR-first

Labs live only in FHIR

In this platform, lab results are exposed only as FHIR DiagnosticReport/Observation — there is no standard-API lab endpoint — which pushes the sidecar to read everything through FHIR R4. That is also the interoperability win: the same doors work against any US Core-conformant EHR, not just this one.

Why no vector search

The whole patient fits

A single patient's prepared fact bundle is small enough to hand the model whole, so retrieval is an exact keyed lookup — no embedding database, no "did we retrieve the right chunk?" failure mode. Scoping to one patient makes the hardest retrieval problem in AI systems simply not arise. (pgvector is installed as headroom for cross-patient/literature queries — deliberately unused in the hot path.)

A derived view is the reason rollback is safe

The fact store holds only what can be regenerated from the record. It is not a migration and not a second database of truth — turn the co-pilot off and the EHR is exactly as it was, "removable without a trace." No live EHR read happens in the chat hot path either: chat reasons over the prepared bundle, which is the source of the latency guarantee and of the clean separation of concerns.

Stability — how a bad change cannot take production down

Layer 1 · branch gate

main is always proven

main is stable/instructor-facing; development lands on a working branch and main only advances by a deliberate, CI-green promotion. A broken commit never reaches production.

Layer 2 · CI

Build what ships

Every push touching sidecar/ runs both test suites, typecheck, and the exact tsc/vite builds the Docker image uses. Green on HEAD is the promotion precondition.

Layer 3 · health-gated deploy

No traffic to a sick boot

Railway won't switch traffic until /health responds, and keeps the previous healthy deployment serving if the new one never becomes healthy. A boot failure cannot replace a working version.

Layer 4 · crash-proof config

loadConfig cannot throw

Every env var .catch()es its own parse failure: a malformed value logs a warning naming the variable and falls back to a safe default, disabling that feature rather than restart-looping the process.

Layer 5 · fail-safe requests

The path never 500s

In off mode a token that fails to verify is ignored and the request proceeds; in enforced mode an unexpected verifier error fails closed with 401 — never a leaked 500.

Rollback

Ten seconds, no code

Each promotion is tagged stable-YYYY-MM-DD. To roll back: redeploy the last green deployment in the Railway console, or check out the latest stable-* tag. The derived-view fact store makes rollback safe by construction.

Observability — you cannot trust what you cannot reconstruct

Tracing

Correlation IDs, end to end

Every request, log line, tool call, and model interaction carries a correlation ID (honoring an inbound x-correlation-id), so the full "what did the agent do, in what order, how long, what did it cost" story reconstructs from logs alone.

Health

Liveness vs readiness, separate

/health is dependency-free process liveness; /ready runs real checks against OpenEMR, the model provider, and Langfuse. They are deliberately not the same endpoint.

Spend

A priced ledger, live

An llm_calls Postgres ledger prices every model call at insert time; GET /api/usage surfaces rolling 24h spend vs. the budget without any external service.

Dashboards

Self-hosted Langfuse + 3 alerts

Emitted spans/generations/scores drive a self-hosted dashboard (traces never leave the boundary) with three defined alerts: p95 latency, error rate, and tool-failure rate — each with a documented response.

The seams to scale are already in place

The current build is single-replica and in-process for demo clarity, but the load-bearing seams exist: async work is a fire-and-forget in-process pipeline behind concurrency + budget guards, with the durable-queue seam (BullMQ on the already-present REDIS_URL) isolated to the prep route; the sidecar is stateless apart from Postgres, so it runs N replicas behind the proxy; migrations are idempotent + advisory-locked so concurrent replicas serialize safely at boot; and auth verifies tokens statelessly (JWKS cached), so no shared session store is needed. Scaling is a queue problem, not a model problem — detailed next.

Cost controls already enforced in code — and the levers left to pull

Enforced today

Guardrails, not good intentions

A rolling $5/day spend guard (429 llm_budget_exceeded), per-call output ceilings (8,192 prep / 1,024 chat), a 10-minute brief-reuse window (no double-pay on panel re-opens), in-flight dedupe (202 already_running), a concurrency cap, and a truncation-never-retried rule so a structural cap-hit can't double the burn. Every call is priced into the ledger at insert time.

Not yet implemented

Where the curve bends

Prompt caching (~10–30% of LLM spend) on the shared extraction prefix across ~14 calls/prep and the brief context across chat turns; Batch API for morning preps (~30–50% of prep spend, since preps precompute overnight). Applied together, plausibly ~$0.10–0.15/visit — unverified until measured.

Part 10 Architecture defense

The hard questions

The challenges a skeptical architecture reviewer would press on — each answered the way the design was actually made: with the alternative that was rejected, and why. A choice defended only by its upside is not defended at all.

Q1 Why a sidecar? Building inside OpenEMR would be simpler to deploy.

Rejected: code inside the EHR's PHP monolith. It would couple the AI's release cycle, failure domain, and performance profile to a 20-year-old codebase, and modern AI tooling support there is thin. Decisively, the audit found OpenEMR has no async job queue — long AI work cannot run inside a request or a synchronous listener. The sidecar owns its own queue; the in-EHR listener does a ≤250 ms fire-and-forget POST and never blocks the tracker save. The sidecar isn't a preference — it's the only shape the platform's performance model allows.

Q2 If the record is cluttered, why not restructure the data instead of layering over it?

Rejected: a data migration. Three grounds. The current structures are load-bearing for billing and certification — changing them risks breaking what pays the clinic. A migration is one-way; a presentation layer can be turned off tomorrow. And the core thesis is that the data isn't the problem — it's a presentation failure, not a data failure. The prepared facts live in a derived view, wipeable and rebuildable, so the EHR remains the single system of record and the layer is "removable without a trace."

Q3 No vector database? Isn't RAG the standard for reading records?

Rejected: embedding search. A single patient's prepared fact bundle fits in the model's context whole, so retrieval is an exact keyed lookup — there is no "did we retrieve the right chunk?" failure mode to engineer around. Scoping to one patient makes the hardest problem in RAG systems simply not arise. pgvector is installed as headroom for the day the corpus outgrows context (cross-patient queries, literature) — deliberately unused in the hot path, not reached for by default.

Q4 Why a general frontier model rather than a medical-tuned or turnkey medical-AI product?

Rejected: turnkey medical AI (scribes, medical-Q&A) and medical-fine-tuned models. The task is not generating medical knowledge — it is faithfully retrieving, organizing, and citing this patient's own record. No vendor product supplies the fact-store, provenance, and EHR-embedding layer where the actual value lives. So the design uses a general model under a healthcare BAA, split two ways: Sonnet 5 as the deep reader (quality, time is free), Haiku 4.5 as the fast responder (the latency targets rule).

Q5 Why TypeScript end to end?

Rejected: rewriting the validated logic in another language. The prototype the design partner already validated holds the two most differentiated pieces of clinical logic — the hydroxychloroquine dose calculator and the injection-interval analyzer — as self-contained JavaScript. In TypeScript they port unchanged; hand-translation would risk introducing errors in exactly the logic this system exists to keep trustworthy. One language also spans service, UI, and tests, so the data contract between them is literally one Zod schema file — it cannot drift.

Q6 One agent, no orchestration framework, no multi-agent — why so plain?

Rejected: an orchestration framework and multi-agent coordination. The control flow is a linear preparation pipeline plus a tool-using chat loop — simple enough to own outright. A framework adds someone else's abstractions to debug and defend without adding capability; extra agents add latency to the one surface where latency is the constraint. Tool chaining is allowed but shallow (fetch → compute → cite), because the expensive multi-step reasoning already happened at preparation time.

Q7 The background preparer reads across patients. Isn't that the very hole you flagged?

It's the design's honest tradeoff, stated plainly rather than hidden. Preparing today's schedule requires reading across the practice's patients before any doctor session exists. The mitigations make it defensible: the credential is read-only, scoped to the preparation pipeline, never touches the interactive surface, and every access is written to OpenEMR's own api_log with identity and timestamp. The alternative — no preparation — collapses the entire latency design. This is a bounded, audited, read-only exposure, not an unmonitored one.

Q8 What actually stops the model from hallucinating a fact into the brief?

A deterministic citation gate — plain code, run between generation and display — verifies every citation resolves to a real record entry; an unsourced claim is blocked by construction and rewritten as absence. Clinical numbers are arithmetic the model presents but never computes, so each is hand-checkable. The honest boundary: this guarantees provenance, not perfect interpretation — a model can still summarize a cited fact clumsily. Mitigations: one-click source on every claim, and faithfulness evals against ground truth on every change.

Q9 Why is a conversational agent the right shape? A dashboard is cheaper.

Rejected: a dashboard. A dashboard shows everything at fixed weight — which is the 20-page template that produced EHR hatred. It cannot decide that this interaction matters for this patient today and 18 other facts don't. The brief is the dashboard for the 80% case; conversation earns its place only for the long tail the brief can't anticipate — cross-referencing history, verification checkpoints, referral archaeology — where each answer sets up the next question and a search box would drop the thread.

Q10 What breaks first at scale, and is it a wall?

Not a wall — a queue problem, not a model problem. The chat service is stateless, so a 500-bed hospital with 300 concurrent users is more identical replicas behind a load balancer plus a wider preparation worker pool; the per-patient work never changes shape. Inflections: ~100 users (single host, as designed) → ~1K (managed DB, multiple instances) → ~10K (dedicated prep workers + prompt caching) → ~100K (multi-tenant isolation, which the per-patient bundle already shards naturally). Cost scales with visits, not seats.

What changes before a physician relies on this

Three things, in order of how much they matter:

1 · data

Real-record ingestion, de-identified

Real charts go behind the de-identification pipeline described in Part 05 — PHI stripped at the clinic edge before any model call. This week is synthetic-only precisely so this can be built deliberately.

2 · availability

High availability

The single-host demo gains real HA — no single point of failure. The seams (stateless sidecar, derived fact store, durable-queue hook) are already in place; this is turning them on.

3 · clinical validation

Validate the exclusions

The brief's omission decisions get clinically validated with the design partner against real charts — because the failure that matters most is not a wrong fact (the gate catches those) but a correctly-cited brief that buried the one thing that mattered.

The one line to leave with

The system may be unavailable; it may never be silently wrong. Every design choice in this document — the citation gate, the arithmetic domain rules, the patient-bound credential, the derived fact store, the fail-closed enforcement, the "missing data shows as missing" failure posture — is an expression of that single rule. It is also the rule a physician, a CISO, and a CTO can each independently verify holds.
