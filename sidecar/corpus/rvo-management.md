## Purpose and scope

This protocol standardizes the practice's workup, macular-edema treatment,
follow-up cadence, and neovascular-complication surveillance for retinal vein
occlusion (RVO). It is our operating standard, grounded in the AAO Preferred
Practice Pattern for Retinal Vein Occlusions and the landmark treatment trials
(BRAVO, CRUISE, COPERNICUS, GALILEO, VIBRANT, GENEVA, SCORE/SCORE2, CVOS). RVO is
usually unilateral at presentation; the fellow eye is assessed for risk factors (OU
applicability).

## Classification and ischemic status

- **Branch RVO (BRVO)** — occlusion of a retinal vein branch, typically at an
  arteriovenous crossing.
- **Central RVO (CRVO)** — occlusion at or near the optic nerve head; hemiretinal RVO
  is a variant.
- **Perfusion status** matters most for complications: **ischemic (nonperfused)**
  versus **non-ischemic (perfused)**. Ischemic CRVO carries the highest
  neovascular-glaucoma risk. Widefield fluorescein angiography grades nonperfusion.

## Systemic and ocular workup

RVO is a marker of vascular risk; the practice coordinates the systemic evaluation
rather than managing it.

| Domain | What to assess |
|--------|----------------|
| Vascular risk factors | Hypertension, diabetes, hyperlipidemia (coordinate with PCP) |
| Ocular | Intraocular pressure and glaucoma status (a CRVO risk factor); gonioscopy |
| Younger patients (< 50) or bilateral/recurrent, no vascular risk factors | Consider hypercoagulable / inflammatory workup |

Elevated IOP is both a CRVO risk factor and a manageable comorbidity; check and
treat it. Refer systemic risk factors to the PCP with a coordination note.

## Macular-edema management (anti-VEGF first-line)

Per AAO PPP and the RVO trials:

- **Anti-VEGF is first-line** for vision-impairing macular edema in both BRVO and
  CRVO (ranibizumab — BRAVO/CRUISE; aflibercept — VIBRANT/COPERNICUS/GALILEO;
  bevacizumab — SCORE2 non-inferior to aflibercept for CRVO edema).
- **Intravitreal corticosteroid** (dexamethasone implant — GENEVA; or triamcinolone —
  SCORE) is an alternative, weighed against steroid-induced IOP rise and cataract.
- **Grid laser** is an adjunct for persistent BRVO macular edema, not first-line.
- Maintenance follows the practice `anti-vegf-treat-and-extend` protocol.

## Follow-up cadence

| Phase / status | Cadence |
|----------------|---------|
| Active macular edema under anti-VEGF | Monthly during initiation, then per treat-and-extend |
| Ischemic CRVO (neovascular surveillance) | Every ~4 weeks for the first ~6 months |
| Non-ischemic, edema resolved | Extend intervals; watch for conversion to ischemic |

Non-ischemic CRVO can convert to ischemic; any drop in vision or new neovascular
sign shortens the interval.

## Neovascular-complication surveillance and treatment

- **Ischemic CRVO** → anterior-segment neovascularization (iris/angle, NVI/NVA) and
  neovascular glaucoma, classically around ~100 days ("90-day glaucoma"). Monitor
  with undilated iris exam and **gonioscopy** at early visits for the first ~6 months.
- **BRVO/ischemic segments** → retinal or disc neovascularization (NVE/NVD) with
  vitreous-hemorrhage risk.
- **Treatment trigger:** for anterior- or posterior-segment neovascularization, apply
  **panretinal (or sector) photocoagulation**; anti-VEGF can temporize acutely. Per
  CVOS, prophylactic PRP before neovascularization is not routine — treat when it appears.

## Referral and action triggers

Flag for physician review / urgent retina evaluation when: new iris or angle
neovascularization; elevated IOP with neovascular glaucoma; vitreous hemorrhage;
retinal neovascularization; or acute vision loss suggesting conversion to ischemic
CRVO. New NVI/NVA is urgent.

## Documentation requirements

Record per eye: RVO type (BRVO/CRVO/hemiretinal), perfusion status, IOP and
gonioscopy findings, macular-edema status and treatments with dates, neovascular
findings, PRP if applied, systemic-coordination note, and the follow-up interval
assigned. Every finding carries laterality and source.
