## Purpose and scope

This protocol defines the practice's treat-and-extend (T&E) regimen for intravitreal
anti-VEGF therapy across neovascular AMD, diabetic macular edema, and macular edema
from retinal vein occlusion. It is our operating standard, grounded in the AAO
Preferred Practice Patterns and published T&E consensus/trial evidence (TREX-AMD,
LUCAS, ALTAIR, ARIES). T&E individualizes the treatment interval to each eye's
activity to minimize both undertreatment and unnecessary injections. Each eye is
managed on its own interval (OU applicability, independent OD/OS intervals).

## Loading phase (establish disease control first)

- Give **3 consecutive monthly injections** (every 4 weeks) as the loading phase.
- If exudation persists, continue monthly until the macula is dry or maximally
  stable before beginning extension.
- Establish a **best-recorded CRT** (central retinal thickness) and best-recorded VA
  during/after loading — these become the reference points for later shortening decisions.

## Extension increments and maximum interval

Once the eye is dry/stable, extend the interval in stepwise increments and cap it.

| Parameter | Value | Condition |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Extension increment | + 2 weeks per stable visit | Applied when extend criteria are met |
| Typical maximum interval | 12–16 weeks | Cap depends on agent and stability; do not exceed without cause |
| Shortening step | − 2 to − 4 weeks | Applied when recurrence criteria are met |

Injection is given at **every** T&E visit (treat-and-extend, not monitor-and-extend);
the interval to the next visit is what changes.

## Criteria to extend the interval (all should hold)

Extend by 2 weeks when the eye is quiet:

- **Dry macula** — no new intraretinal or subretinal fluid on OCT, or stable minimal fluid.
- **Stable CRT** — no meaningful rise from the eye's best-recorded thickness.
- **No new hemorrhage** on exam/imaging.
- **Stable visual acuity** — no significant drop from best-recorded VA.

## Criteria to shorten the interval (any one triggers)

Shorten by 2–4 weeks when activity recurs:

- **Recurrent or new fluid** (intraretinal or subretinal) on OCT.
- **CRT rise > ~50 µm** from the eye's best-recorded thickness.
- **New retinal or subretinal hemorrhage.**
- **Visual-acuity drop** (e.g., a loss of roughly ≥ 5–10 letters / ≥ 1 line) attributable to activity.

When only one borderline criterion is present, holding the current interval
(maintain, do not extend) is acceptable pending the next visit.

## When to switch anti-VEGF agents

Consider switching agents (or shortening to fixed monthly) for a suboptimal or
non-responder pattern:

- **Persistent fluid despite ≥ 3 consecutive monthly injections** of the current agent.
- **Inability to extend** beyond a short interval (e.g., recurrent activity at every
  attempted extension) over multiple cycles.
- **Tachyphylaxis** — progressive loss of a previously good response.
- **Intolerance** or a safety signal (e.g., intraocular inflammation).

Document the reason for any switch and re-establish control (return toward the
loading approach) on the new agent before resuming extension.

## Referral and action triggers

Flag for physician review when: activity recurs at the maximum interval; vision
drops despite treatment; a switch is being considered; or a complication is
suspected (endophthalmitis, sustained IOP rise, retinal tear/detachment). Suspected
endophthalmitis after an injection is an emergency, not a routine flag.

## Documentation requirements

Record per eye and per visit: agent and dose given, current interval and the next
interval assigned, the extend/maintain/shorten decision and which criterion drove
it, OCT fluid status and CRT versus best-recorded CRT, VA versus best-recorded VA,
and any agent switch with its rationale. Every value carries laterality and source.
